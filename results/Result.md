

**Analysis Results & Conclusion - ATP5F1A**

**Project:** ATP5F1A Longevity Analysis using ESM2  
**Date:** June 2026  
**Number of species:** 11 mammals

### Key Findings

- ATP5F1A is a **highly conserved** protein across mammals, with only approximately **35 variable positions** out of 553 amino acids (~6.3% variability).
- Most sequence variations are **phylogenetically driven**, primarily distinguishing rodents (mouse, rat, hamster) from other mammalian orders.
- No clear or consistent pattern was observed between **long-lived species** (e.g., Human, African elephant, Naked mole-rat) and **short-lived species** (e.g., Mouse, Rat, Hamster).
- Embedding-based analyses (PCA, Cosine Distance Heatmap, Hierarchical Clustering) show clustering patterns that align with evolutionary relationships rather than lifespan categories.
- The Little brown bat (*Myotis lucifugus*) was used as a representative for bats due to the unavailability of Brandt’s bat (*Myotis brandtii*) sequence. It did not exhibit any distinctive longevity-related features.

### Final Conclusion

Despite its critical role in mitochondrial ATP production, **ATP5F1A does not show significant evolutionary signals associated with species lifespan** in this dataset. The protein appears to be under strong purifying selection, maintaining high conservation across mammals with vastly different lifespans. 

The observed differences are better explained by phylogenetic distance than by longevity mechanisms.

This finding is consistent with the previous analysis of ATP5F1B and suggests that not all subunits of the ATP synthase complex carry detectable lifespan-related variations at the sequence level.

**Future directions:**  
Further investigation of other ATP synthase subunits, functional studies of variable sites, and integration with structural or expression data may provide deeper insights.
